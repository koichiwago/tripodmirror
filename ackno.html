Acknowledgements

This work would not have been possible without the efforts of many others. First, I would like to thank my advisor, Dan Rugar, for his continuous support. I deeply appreciate not only the valuable scientific and technical guidance he has provided as an experienced experimentalist, but also his warmhearted personality, which created a positive work atmosphere for me and provided effective motivation during the course of this work. I have been particularly fortunate to be able to work in his laboratory for the past four years. I would like to thank Prof. Cal Quate for giving me such a fortunate opportunity. When I first met him in Japan, I was preparing to apply for graduate study in the U.S., and he encouraged me to apply to Stanford. This provided one of the main motivations for my coming to Stanford, among, of course, other things such as the nice climate. I also appreciate his continuous support after I arrived at Stanford, and I am grateful that he suggested that I work on a truly interesting project with Dan. I also thank Prof. Tom Kenny for critically reading my thesis.

At IBM Almaden Research Center, I have been fortunate to work with many nice people. Discussions with Nino Yannoni on various topics including magnetic resonance and stylistic English writing have always enlightened me. Without his effort to find the right sample material for the right experiment, none of the experiments in this thesis would have been possible. Othmar Zueger seemed to know every hidden trick necessary to make experiments work. I learned a great deal from him, from how to glue samples onto cantilevers to how to deconvolve three-dimensional force maps to reconstruct real-space images. I would like to thank him for his countless contributions to this work, especially to Chapters 3 and 4. Storrs Hoen's vitality always amazed me and I would not have been able to complete this work without his kind assurance that I would not ruin my carrier as a graduate student even if I inadvertently were to break a custom-made cantilever that was a result of his hard work. Working with Jurgen Wegener was also fun and he gave me more insights into magnetic resonance than I could have imagined. The time I worked together and shared an office with David Botkin was invaluable. He always provided me with a very different view of looking at problems. I would like to thank him particularly for his contributions to the work described in Chapters 5 and 6, and for delicious apricots. Discussions with Hans-Martin Vieth on what seems to me the mysterious behavior of spins helped me to understand the complexity of magnetic resonance. Ray Kendrick's technical help including the lecture on high-frequency circuitry was priceless. Once again, I would like to thank all these people who have supported me through the past years.

I would also like to thank John Mamin and Bruce Terris for getting me started when I had no idea where a screwdriver was in the lab, for letting me borrow their equipment, and for conversations that have made life at Almaden enjoyable. I thank Tom Albrecht not only for stimulating discussions we had at Almaden but also for his contribution to the earlier version of the apparatus, on which the microscope described in this thesis is based. I also appreciate the technical support of many people at Almaden, including the staff at the model shop, Bob Fontana, Linda Lane, Richard Campbell, and Tim Reiley.

His thoroughness in his work has always amazed me. I also thank Toshiki Hirano for his assistance in sample fabrication.
